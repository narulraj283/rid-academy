How to Drop PPO Plans: A Complete Guide for Dental Practices

Learn the exact process used by 10,000+ dental practices to successfully drop PPO plans, retain patients, and grow revenue. This comprehensive guide covers financial analysis, communication strategies, timing, and alternatives.

Dropping PPO plans is one of the most significant decisions a dental practice owner can make. It's also one of the most intimidating. The question that keeps many dentists awake at night is simple: "What if I lose all my patients?"

The truth is, most dentists who follow a structured approach and communicate effectively retain 70-85% of their patients when dropping a PPO plan. Many practices report that after an initial 2-3 month dip, revenue exceeds pre-PPO levels within 12 months. This guide walks you through exactly how to do it.

1. Why Drop PPO Plans?

Before diving into the "how," it's worth understanding the "why." PPO write-offs represent lost revenue that impacts your practice's sustainability and your personal income.

The Financial Reality

Most dental practices lose 42-45% of their potential revenue to PPO write-offs. For a single-doctor practice generating $500,000 in annual revenue, that's approximately $210,000-$225,000 in lost income annually. Over a 20-year career, that's $4-4.5 million in foregone income.

Additionally, PPO contracts often restrict your ability to:

  • Set your own fees
  • Control your schedule
  • Choose your patient base
  • Practice as you see fit
  • Build long-term wealth

The Market Shift

According to recent data from the RID Academy community, 31.8% of US dentists now plan to drop PPO plans in the next 3 years. This represents a fundamental shift in how dentistry is being practiced. Patients increasingly understand and accept fee-for-service dentistry, especially when it's paired with transparent communication and value-based alternatives like membership plans.

2. Conduct a Comprehensive Financial Analysis

You can't make an informed decision about dropping PPO plans without understanding your exact financial situation. This section walks you through the analysis you need to do.

Step 1: Calculate Your PPO Write-Off Rate

Your write-off rate is the percentage of potential revenue you lose to PPO fee schedules. To calculate this:

  1. Pull your practice management software reports for the last 12 months
  2. For each procedure code, record the full fee and the PPO-allowed amount
  3. Calculate the difference (this is your write-off)
  4. Divide total write-offs by total potential revenue (fees at full rates)
  5. Multiply by 100 to get your percentage

Use our PPO Calculator: If manual calculation feels overwhelming, use the free RID Academy PPO Write-Off Calculator to get exact numbers in minutes.

Step 2: Break Down Revenue by Plan

Not all PPO plans are created equal. Some have much worse fee schedules than others. Create a breakdown:

  • Total revenue per PPO plan (last 12 months)
  • Percentage of total practice revenue for each plan
  • Number of active patients per plan
  • Average patient lifetime value per plan
  • Write-off rate per plan

This breakdown will help you identify which plans are worth dropping first. Plans with high write-off rates and lower patient volumes are typically the best candidates for initial termination.

Step 3: Project Cash Flow Impact

Dropping a PPO plan will initially impact your cash flow. Be realistic about this. If a PPO plan represents 15% of your revenue and you retain 75% of those patients, you'll see an initial 3.75% revenue reduction. However:

  • Patients switching to fee-for-service typically pay higher fees (at your full rate, not PPO rates)
  • Many patients will join your membership plan, creating predictable monthly revenue
  • Your per-patient profitability increases significantly
  • Insurance write-offs stop, improving your actual cash collection

The key is to ensure you have adequate cash reserves (typically 3-6 months of operating expenses) before dropping a significant plan.

3. Assess Your Practice Readiness

Not every practice is ready to drop PPO plans. Being financially prepared is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to assess operational and marketing readiness.

Operational Readiness

Patient communication capability: Can you effectively communicate with patients about fee changes? Do you have systems in place for direct mail, email, and phone outreach?

Team alignment: Does your clinical and business team understand the decision and support it? Staff who aren't aligned will struggle with patient conversations and will damage the transition.

Practice management software: Can your software handle fee-for-service billing? Can it manage membership plan tracking? If not, you may need to upgrade or implement additional systems.

Patient base characteristics: What percentage of your patients are self-employed or have high household income? These patients are more likely to embrace fee-for-service dentistry. Conversely, if your patient base is primarily low-income or heavily dependent on insurance, the transition will be more difficult.

Marketing Readiness

Dropping a PPO plan requires active marketing to communicate the change and drive new patient acquisition to replace any lost revenue. Can you:

  • Update your website and online presence to explain your fee structure?
  • Create educational content about the benefits of fee-for-service dentistry?
  • Run patient retention campaigns to minimize defection?
  • Invest in new patient acquisition (direct mail, digital ads, referral programs)?
  • Manage your online reputation and reviews?

If you're not prepared to invest in marketing during the transition, your success rate drops significantly.

4. Develop Your Patient Communication Strategy

Communication is the single most important factor determining your success in dropping a PPO plan. Poor communication leads to patient defection and negative reviews. Excellent communication leads to retention and often improves patient satisfaction.

Key Messaging Principles

Focus on benefits, not restrictions: Don't tell patients "we're dropping your insurance." Instead, explain what they gain: better quality care, more time per appointment, more treatment options, and lower overall costs through your membership plan.

Emphasize continuity: Reassure patients that you're still their dentist and that care will continue seamlessly. The only change is the payment method.

Highlight value: Explain why you're making this change (to provide better care, to reduce barriers to treatment, to offer more affordable options). Patients respond better when they understand your reasoning.

Provide solutions: Don't just tell patients about fee changes. Offer your membership plan or payment plan options immediately.

Communication Timeline

Most PPO contracts require 30-90 days written notice before termination takes effect. Use this time strategically:

60-90 Days Before Termination
Internal team training: Brief your entire team on the change, the reasoning, and how to respond to patient questions. Script common objections and responses.
45-60 Days Before Termination
Initial patient communication: Send a letter to all patients explaining the change. Mail it, don't email (email gets lost). Include information about your membership plan and payment options.
30-45 Days Before Termination
Follow-up phone calls: Call key patients, especially high-value ones, to discuss the change directly. Answer questions. Emphasize that you want them to continue as patients.
15-30 Days Before Termination
In-office signage and discussions: Post information about the change in your waiting room. Train front desk to discuss it with every patient. Schedule consultations for patients with questions.
7 Days Before Termination
Final reminder: Send a follow-up email to all patients confirming the effective date and reminding them of membership plan options.
After Termination
Ongoing engagement: Continue educating patients about fee-for-service benefits. Share patient stories. Optimize your membership plan based on feedback.

5. Choose Your Transition Method: Gradual vs. Cold Turkey

There are two main approaches to dropping PPO plans: gradual reduction (dropping one plan every 3-6 months) and cold turkey (dropping all plans at once, or a large portion simultaneously).

The Gradual Reduction Approach

Best for: First-time droppers, practices with tight cash flow, practices with diverse patient bases

Advantages:

  • Lower risk: You can observe results before dropping the next plan
  • Team readiness: Staff learns the process incrementally
  • Patient communication: Easier to manage patient transitions one at a time
  • Revenue stability: Less dramatic cash flow impact
  • Learning opportunities: You can refine your approach based on each transition

Disadvantages:

  • Longer timeline: Total transition takes 18-24 months instead of 3-6
  • Extended effort: You're managing multiple transitions simultaneously as you progress
  • Continued write-offs: You're still losing money on remaining PPO plans

The Cold Turkey Approach

Best for: Experienced practices, practices with strong cash reserves, practices with high patient satisfaction

Advantages:

  • Faster implementation: Complete transition in 3-6 months
  • Cleaner break: No ongoing PPO obligations or write-offs
  • Market impact: Bold move demonstrates confidence in your practice
  • Team alignment: No confusion about which plans are active

Disadvantages:

  • Higher risk: Significant cash flow impact if patient retention is lower than expected
  • Team stress: Overwhelming for staff, especially front office
  • Patient confusion: Managing communication with all affected patients simultaneously is complex
  • Reputation risk: If handled poorly, can lead to negative reviews and patient defection

Our Recommendation

For most practices, the gradual reduction approach is preferable. Drop your worst-performing PPO plan first (lowest revenue, highest write-off rate), monitor results for 3 months, then drop the next plan. This approach minimizes risk while building team and practice confidence.

Once you've successfully dropped 2-3 plans, you may feel confident enough to drop remaining plans more quickly or simultaneously.

6. Plan Your Membership Alternative

Dropping PPO plans without offering patients an affordable alternative is a recipe for disaster. In-house membership plans are the most effective solution.

What Is a Membership Plan?

A membership plan is a monthly or annual fee that gives patients access to specific benefits. It's not insurance—it's a direct relationship between you and your patient. Common membership plan structures include:

Essential Plan ($29-50/month)
2 cleanings per year, 1 exam per year, 1 full mouth X-ray annually, 10% discount on other services
Premier Plan ($79-120/month)
Everything in Essential plus: fluoride treatment, sealants, endodontic evaluation, 15-20% discount on other services
Concierge Plan ($129-200+/month)
Everything in Premier plus: priority scheduling, extended appointment times, cosmetic consultations, 25-30% discount on major services

How to Price Your Membership Plan

Pricing depends on your market, your services, and your goals. Use this framework:

  1. Calculate your target annual revenue per patient: What would you like to make from a typical patient in annual membership fees and treatment revenue combined?
  2. Estimate annual treatment costs: Add up the value of the benefits you're offering annually
  3. Set membership fee: Price so that membership fees + average treatment revenue = your target
  4. Monitor and adjust: Track enrollment and profitability, adjust pricing after 6 months if needed

Use the Membership Pricing Calculator: Try the RID Academy Membership Pricing Calculator to find optimal pricing for your practice based on your market, services, and goals.

Enrollment Strategy

Don't wait until after you've dropped the PPO plan to launch your membership. Start enrolling patients 60 days before termination. Strategies:

  • Offer founding member discounts (first 50 members get 3 months free, etc.)
  • Highlight savings vs. insurance copays
  • Make enrollment super easy (one-page form, paperless if possible)
  • Enroll high-value patients personally
  • Offer family plans at discounted rates
  • Continue enrollment campaigns after the PPO termination date

7. Prepare Your Marketing Campaign

Dropping a PPO plan creates a temporary vacuum in your patient base. You need an aggressive marketing plan to fill that gap and drive new patient acquisition during the transition period.

Pre-Transition Marketing (60-90 Days Before)

Website update: Update your insurance/payment page to explain your fee-for-service model and membership plan. Create a new landing page focused on the benefits of direct payment.

Educational content: Create blog posts, videos, or social media content explaining the benefits of fee-for-service dentistry. Address common questions and concerns.

Email campaign: Launch an email sequence explaining the transition and membership plan to your current patient base.

Direct mail: Send a postcard to your patient base about the upcoming changes and invitation to membership.

During-Transition Marketing (30-60 Days After)

New patient acquisition: Increase spending on Google Local Services Ads, Facebook/Instagram ads, and local marketing to drive new patient appointments.

Referral incentives: Ask current patients for referrals. Offer incentives (free cleaning, discount on services) for new patient referrals.

Community events: Host a patient appreciation event celebrating the transition. Use it to educate new patients about your approach.

Review management: Monitor Google, Yelp, and other platforms closely. Respond to negative reviews professionally. Ask satisfied patients to leave reviews.

Post-Transition Marketing (3-12 Months After)

Patient success stories: Create case studies or testimonials from patients who've chosen to stay or join during the transition.

Ongoing new patient marketing: Continue investing in new patient acquisition while you rebuild your patient base.

Membership growth: Continue enrolling patients in your membership plan. Optimize the enrollment process based on what you've learned.

  • Begin membership enrollment push
  • Update website with fee-for-service information

  • Follow-up phone calls with high-value patients

  • Monitor online reviews and respond to feedback

  • PPO plan termination becomes effective
  • Begin billing all affected patients at full fee or membership rate

9. Frequently Asked Questions

How much notice do I need to give to drop a PPO plan?

Most PPO contracts require 30-90 days written notice before termination takes effect. Some plans may require up to 6 months notice. Check your specific contract terms or contact the plan's network relations department to confirm requirements.

What percentage of patients will I lose?

On average, practices retain 70-85% of patients when dropping a PPO plan. The retention rate depends on: the quality of your patient relationships, your communication strategy, your membership plan alternatives, local competition, and patient demographics. High-income patient bases typically have higher retention rates.

Should I drop all PPO plans at once or gradually?

For most practices, a gradual approach is better. Drop your worst-performing plan first, monitor results for 3 months, then drop the next plan. This minimizes risk, allows your team to learn the process, and gives you time to refine your approach. Once you've successfully dropped 2-3 plans, you may feel confident dropping remaining plans more quickly.

How long will my revenue dip last?

Most practices see an initial 2-3 month revenue dip after dropping a PPO plan. However, with effective patient communication, a strong membership plan, and aggressive new patient marketing, revenue typically returns to pre-PPO levels within 6-12 months. Many practices report exceeding previous revenue levels within 12 months due to higher per-patient profitability.

What happens to patients with pending treatment plans?

Most PPO contracts allow you to honor benefits for patients with pending treatment plans (typically benefits in effect as of the termination date). Consult your contract and the PPO company to understand what's covered. Communicate clearly with patients about how the transition affects their treatment plans.

Can I negotiate better PPO fees instead of dropping?

Negotiation is possible but difficult. Most PPO contracts include "most favored nation" clauses that prevent you from offering better rates to the plan. Additionally, PPO companies rarely agree to significant rate increases. Many practices find that the effort required to negotiate is better spent on dropping underperforming plans and building direct-pay relationships with patients.

What's the best way to communicate with patients about dropping their insurance plan?

Use a multi-channel approach: (1) Direct mail letter 60 days before termination explaining the change and benefits, (2) Phone calls to high-value patients 45-60 days before, (3) In-office signage and discussions 30 days before, (4) Email follow-ups 7 days before, (5) Ongoing messaging after the transition. Focus on benefits, not restrictions. Emphasize continuity of care and membership plan alternatives.

What metrics should I track during and after the transition?

Track: (1) Patient retention rate (percent of affected patients who stay), (2) Average transaction value (fee-for-service vs. insurance), (3) Monthly revenue (before and after), (4) Membership plan enrollment and pricing, (5) New patient acquisition (to replace lost revenue), (6) Cash flow and accounts receivable aging, (7) Patient satisfaction scores, (8) Online reviews and reputation. Monitor these metrics for at least 12 months post-transition.

Will dropping a PPO plan hurt my reputation or patient reviews?

Potentially, but only if communication is poor. Practices that communicate proactively, emphasize benefits, and offer membership plan alternatives typically maintain or improve their reputation. In fact, many patients respect the decision and see it as evidence of your commitment to quality care. Monitor online reviews closely during the transition and respond professionally to any negative reviews.

Key Takeaways

  • Financial analysis is essential: Calculate your exact PPO write-off rate and project the financial impact before making any decision.
  • Communication is everything: Poor communication leads to patient defection and negative reviews. Excellent communication leads to retention and often improves satisfaction.
  • Gradual reduction is lower risk: For most practices, dropping one PPO plan every 3-6 months is better than going cold turkey.
  • Membership plans are the answer: In-house membership plans provide patients with affordable alternatives and create predictable monthly revenue.
  • Marketing investment is crucial: You need to actively drive new patient acquisition during the transition to replace any lost revenue.
  • Timing matters: Provide 60-90 days advance notice to patients. Use this time strategically for communication and preparation.
  • Track everything: Monitor patient retention, revenue, membership enrollment, and satisfaction throughout the transition and for 12 months after.
