The Future of Dentistry: Why Fee-for-Service Is the Path Forward

11 min read Strategic Perspective

The future of dentistry belongs to practitioners who practice as professionals rather than service providers in an industry. Understanding the distinction between dentistry as a profession versus an industry is crucial for your career trajectory. Explore what genuine fee-for-service dentistry means, discover the characteristics of top 1% practices, and understand why strategic growth is the path to sustainable success and professional fulfillment.

Dentistry: Profession or Industry?

This fundamental question shapes everything about your practice. Is dentistry a profession—a calling based on expertise, ethics, and professional judgment? Or has it become an industry—a business driven by financial metrics and standardized protocols?

The distinction matters profoundly. When dentistry is practiced as a profession, dentists make clinical decisions based on what's best for the patient. They charge what their expertise is worth. They practice the dentistry they were trained to provide. They build relationships of trust with patients who value their professional judgment.

When dentistry becomes an industry, decisions are driven by insurance company rules, contracted fee schedules, and volume metrics. Dentists become service providers executing someone else's protocols. They're constrained by insurance limitations rather than guided by their professional expertise.

Insurance's Influence on Professional Practice

PPO plans have fundamentally shaped modern dentistry. By imposing significant financial discounts, insurance companies have influenced dentists to adopt a volume-based practice model—see more patients in less time, accept lower fees, and prioritize insurance billing over patient care. This transforms dentists from professionals into service providers.

The future belongs to practitioners who reclaim dentistry's professional identity. This begins with understanding that insurance dependence prevents professional practice.

Understanding True Fee-for-Service

Fee-for-service doesn't mean charging exorbitant fees or abandoning insurance-insured patients. Rather, it means:

Core FFS Principles

  • Charge Your Full Fees: You set your fees based on your expertise, costs, and business needs. You don't let insurance companies dictate your worth.
  • Receive Fees Directly: Patients pay you. You don't depend on insurance companies to reimburse you. This removes the perverse incentive to adapt your care to what insurance will cover.
  • Help Patients with Insurance Benefits: If patients have insurance, you can still help them navigate their benefits. You can complete claims. You can explain their coverage. But you don't adjust your treatment recommendations based on insurance coverage.
  • Maintain Professional Boundaries: Insurance companies don't dictate what treatment you recommend. You do. Your recommendations are based on what's clinically optimal, not what insurance will pay for.
  • Practice with Integrity: You're not constrained by insurance company rules that often contradict what you know is best for patients. You practice dentistry as you learned it.

Many fear FFS dentistry requires abandoning insurance-insured patients. This is false. FFS practices can work with insured patients. The difference is that insurance benefits influence the patient's payment method, not your clinical recommendations.

The Dilemma of Insurance-Driven Patients

Even with exceptional clinical skills and excellent protocols, insurance-driven patients often don't value what you do. They ask repeatedly, "Will insurance cover this?" They decline comprehensive treatment if insurance won't pay. They shop around based on fees rather than quality.

This creates a painful dynamic. You can provide impeccable dentistry, but if the patient has an insurance mindset, they won't value it or accept treatment. You're left trying to convince insurance-mindset patients to accept ideal care, all while being constrained by insurance fee schedules.

The solution isn't to work harder at convincing these patients. It's to attract patients who value quality over cost. Patients who choose your practice because of your reputation, your expertise, and your patient-centered care. Patients who pay full fees because they understand the value you provide.

Top 1% Practices: What Sets Them Apart

What distinguishes top 1% practices from the rest? It's not just production volume or patient count. Top practices share specific characteristics:

Seven Characteristics of Top 1% Practices

  • Ideal Patient Base: Top practices attract patients who value quality, follow recommendations, and refer others. They've intentionally built a patient base aligned with their practice vision.
  • Healthy Work-Life Balance: Top 1% dentists aren't working 10-hour days just to keep up. They've structured their practices to allow reasonable working hours while maintaining high productivity.
  • High-Performance Teams: Top practices have team members who are engaged, trained, and aligned with practice vision. Team members aren't just processing insurance—they're delivering exceptional patient experiences.
  • Comprehensive Services: Top practices offer high-value services beyond basic dentistry. Implants, adult orthodontics, cosmetic dentistry, and other advanced services expand revenue potential and attract ideal patients.
  • Fee-for-Service Model: Most top practices have reduced or eliminated PPO insurance. They control their fees and their destiny.
  • Strategic Growth: Rather than random growth, top practices grow strategically by adding services, attracting ideal patients, and continuously improving operations.
  • Professional Culture: Top practices maintain professional standards and resist becoming service factories. They practice dentistry as a profession.

The Power of Strategic Growth

Growth is essential for any business, but all growth isn't equal. Random growth creates chaos. Strategic growth creates value.

Why Strategic Growth Matters

Inflation and rising costs mean that standing still isn't an option. Your costs increase annually. If your income doesn't increase proportionally, your profit margin shrinks. Strategic growth ensures that your practice's income and value continue to rise.

Strategies for Sustainable Growth

  • Resign from Low-Fee PPO Plans: When you resign from plans with contracted fees significantly below your full fees, you retain 45-50% more revenue. Even if you lose some patients, the revenue increase is substantial.
  • Add High-Value Services: Implants, orthodontics, and cosmetic dentistry significantly increase revenue per patient. Patients who value cosmetic enhancements and tooth replacement often have fewer insurance limitations.
  • Improve Operational Efficiency: Better scheduling, reduced cancellations, and optimized team protocols increase production without increasing working hours.
  • Enhance Case Acceptance: Better diagnostic communication and treatment discussions increase the percentage of recommended treatment that patients accept.
  • Build Referral Systems: Strategic relationships with other professionals can generate referrals that accelerate growth.

The Sustainability Factor

Strategic growth is sustainable because it's intentional and manageable. You're not growing through volume alone (which leads to burnout) but through value creation. You're adding services, improving systems, and attracting better patients—all sustainable elements.

The Transition Path

Moving from an insurance-dependent practice to a professional FFS practice is a journey, not an overnight transformation. The path generally follows this progression:

  1. Understand Your Current Reality: Know your actual fees, insurance discounts, and what each plan costs you. This creates motivation for change.
  2. Develop FFS Infrastructure: Create systems for marketing, patient communication, and treatment case presentation that work without insurance as your primary patient acquisition tool.
  3. Strategic Resignations: Begin with the most unprofitable plans. As your referral systems strengthen, add more resignations.
  4. Service Enhancement: Add high-value services that attract ideal patients and increase practice value.
  5. Team Alignment: Ensure your team understands and supports the new practice model.
  6. Sustainability: Establish systems that maintain excellence and continued growth without burnout.

The Future Vision

The future of dentistry belongs to practitioners who practice professionally, serve ideal patients, and grow strategically. Insurance companies will continue evolving, but the fundamental shift toward fee-for-service is inevitable. Those who make the transition early position themselves as industry leaders while improving their professional satisfaction and personal prosperity.
